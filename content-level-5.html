Level 5: Changing Thoughts

So far we have focused on changing your behaviours and routines related to sleep. For some people it is also important to change the way they think about sleep. This section focuses on strategies for changing some commonly held misconceptions.

Unhelpful thoughts can happen automatically, outside of awareness. The first step to changing these thoughts is to begin to notice them when they happen. The next step is to try replacing unhelpful thoughts with more helpful alternatives.

Let’s consider Mary’s situation. She finds herself awake at 3:00a.m. She is worried about her ability to function tomorrow.

“I need to find a way to fall asleep. I won’t be able to function tomorrow”.

What are some more helpful ways of thinking about her situation?

She thinks about the evidence that supports her beliefs. She remembers that she has been able to function after a poor night’s sleep in the past. This allows her to replace her unhelpful thoughts with more helpful ones:

“There is no point worrying about this now. Sometimes I can still function after a poor night’s sleep”.

Let’s try challenging some other examples of thoughts people with insomnia commonly believe.

“It is necessary to get at least 8 hours of sleep each night.”
Some people feel refreshed after 7 or 8 hours, but others thrive with only 6.5 hours. The amount of sleep we need varies from person to person. Trying to achieve unrealistic goals generally just increases feelings of worry and anxiety. Your sleep reports can help determine what a realistic expectation is for you.
“I should be able to wake up feeling refreshed and full of energy every day.”
Energy levels vary from day to day. Even sound sleepers occasionally wake up without feeling completely rested. It is normal to have days when you don’t feel fully refreshed.
“My partner falls asleep as soon as his or her head hits the pillow and stays asleep throughout the night. I should be able to do that too.”
Everyone is different. Just because one person can fall asleep and stay asleep easily doesn’t mean it will be that way for everyone else. Instead of focusing on other people, try to remember the strategies that have helped you improve your sleep.
“I feel irritable, depressed, or anxious during the day because I did not sleep well last night.”
Sleep is one reason for being in a bad mood, but there are lots of other reasons too. Work, health, and relationships are other common sources of stress that affect how we feel throughout the day. Blaming sleeplessness for all difficulties in daytime functioning is not particularly helpful.
“I need to avoid or cancel important social and family obligations after a poor night’s sleep. This is the only way I can manage the results of a poor night’s sleep.”
Changing your plans based on how well you slept can mean that you miss out on important parts of life. Someone who does this often may find that his or her entire life begins to be organized around sleep.
Try not to place too much emphasis on sleep. Instead, focus on doing the things you enjoy even if your sleep wasn’t perfect the night before.

Have you ever felt this way? The problem here is that lying awake trying to sleep rarely makes it easier fall asleep. This way of thinking also tends to make people feel helpless. As if there is nothing they can do to improve their sleep. This is simply not true. The key is to try the right things. If you can’t fall asleep within 15 minutes, try getting out of bed to do something else. Continue practicing the techniques you have learned so far (sleep restriction, sleep hygiene, relaxation, etc.).

Did any of these examples seem especially true for you? Maybe these are thoughts you have had in the past. Maybe you have other negative thoughts you would like to change. Practice challenging the unhelpful thoughts you have in your everyday life.

• What is the evidence that supports this idea?
• What is the evidence against this idea?
• Is there another explanation?
• What is the worst that could happen? Could I live with that?
• What is the best that could happen?
• What is the most realistic outcome?
• What would I tell a friend if he or she was in the same situation?
